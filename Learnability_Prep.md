# Learnability Prep: ROAS, ACOS, and TACOS

This guide breaks down the three core metrics of Amazon Advertising—ROAS, ACOS, and TACOS—explaining what they measure, how they relate, and how to discuss them naturally in a voice note.

---

## 1. Core Concepts & Definitions

### Concept 1: ROAS (Return on Ad Spend)
* **What it measures**: The efficiency of your advertising spend. For every rupee you put into ads, how many rupees of sales do you get back?
* **Formula**:
  $$\text{ROAS} = \frac{\text{Ad-Attributed Sales Revenue}}{\text{Total Ad Spend}}$$
  *(Note: It is expressed as a multiplier or ratio, e.g., 4.0x or 4:1).*
* **Why a brand spending ₹15L/month cares**: 
  A ₹15L/month ad budget is a massive cash outlay. The brand needs to know if their ads are generating incremental sales or if they are throwing money down a black hole. If their ROAS is 4.0, that ₹15L of ad spend is generating ₹60L in revenue. If ROAS drops to 1.5, that same spend generates only ₹22.5L, which likely won't cover product and platform costs, resulting in a heavy cash loss.
* **What a "good" ROAS looks like**:
  There is no single "good" number because it depends on product margins. 
  * **Low Margin Categories (e.g., Electronics, Grocery)**: Margins are thin (15–25%). A "good" ROAS needs to be high (e.g., 5.0 to 8.0) to cover COGS and fees.
  * **High Margin Categories (e.g., Skincare, Supplements, Fashion)**: Margins are fat (60–80%). A brand can survive and remain highly profitable even at a lower ROAS (e.g., 2.0 to 3.0) because the underlying unit profitability is high.

### Concept 2: ACOS (Advertising Cost of Sale)
* **What it measures**: The percentage of ad-attributed revenue spent on advertising. It is the direct inverse of ROAS.
* **Formula**:
  $$\text{ACOS} = \frac{\text{Total Ad Spend}}{\text{Ad-Attributed Sales Revenue}} \times 100$$
  $$\text{ACOS} = \frac{1}{\text{ROAS}} \times 100$$
* **Why they are inverses**: 
  ROAS asks "What did we get *for* what we spent?" (Sales / Spend). ACOS asks "What percentage *of* what we got did we spend?" (Spend / Sales). 
  * If ROAS = 4.0 $\rightarrow$ ACOS = $1/4 = 25\%$.
  * If ROAS = 2.0 $\rightarrow$ ACOS = $1/2 = 50\%$.
* **When to tolerate a high ACOS**:
  * **New Product Launch**: During a launch, you need to feed the Amazon algorithm search history and reviews. You will bid aggressively to win placements. An ACOS of 70% or 100% is acceptable here because you are buying "data and momentum," not short-term profit.
  * **Brand Defense**: Bidding on your own brand terms to block competitors from stealing your loyal customers. This prevents customer leakage.
* **When a high ACOS is a problem**:
  * **Mature Products**: If a product has been live for a year and still has an ACOS of 55% while your product margin is 40%, you are bleeding cash on every ad-driven sale without building incremental organic growth.

### Concept 3: TACOS (Total Advertising Cost of Sale)
* **What it measures**: The percentage of **total revenue** (both organic and paid) spent on advertising. It measures the true health and profitability of the overall business.
* **Formula**:
  $$\text{TACOS} = \frac{\text{Total Ad Spend}}{\text{Total Revenue (Organic + Ad-Attributed)}} \times 100$$
* **Why TACOS is a better health metric than ACOS for long-term decisions**:
  ACOS is a narrow sandbox. It only looks at ad sales. But advertising on Amazon has a **halo effect**: winning ad sales improves search rankings, which increases organic (free) traffic and sales.
  * If ad spend stays flat but organic sales double, your **ACOS stays flat**, but your **TACOS drops**. This shows that your advertising is successfully seeding organic growth.
  * If you focus only on ACOS, you might shut down an ad campaign that has a "poor" 45% ACOS, only to watch your organic sales drop by 50% because the product lost its best search rankings. TACOS catches this connection.
* **What happens to TACOS as organic sales grow alongside paid**:
  TACOS shrinks. If organic sales grow, total revenue grows. Since ad spend is in the numerator and total revenue is in the denominator, TACOS goes down. A shrinking TACOS indicates a compounding brand that is gaining organic equity and becoming more profitable over time.

## 2. 5+ Minute Learnability Voice Note Script

### Preparation Guidelines
* **Tone**: Energetic, professional, conversational. Imagine you are explaining this to a peer over coffee.
* **Delivery**: Do not read this word-for-word. Use it as a structured guide. Let your natural pauses and reflections show through.
* **Key Goal**: Connect the dots and use the custom example ("EcoBeans Coffee") to show genuine understanding.

---

### Voice Note Script Outline

#### [0:00 - 1:00] Intro & The Core Framework
* "Hey there! I want to break down how we think about Amazon advertising economics, specifically through three core metrics: ROAS, ACOS, and TACOS. Before diving into the formulas, it’s best to visualize them as different lenses on the same dollar.
* Let's use a concrete example. Imagine we run a premium organic coffee brand on Amazon India called **EcoBeans**. We sell a premium bag of coffee beans for ₹600.
* Now, to get sales, we run sponsored ads. Suppose in a given month, we spend **₹1,20,000** on ads. Those ads directly generate **₹4,80,000** in sales. We also have a lot of customers who just search for us or find us naturally—our organic sales—which bring in another **₹7,20,000**.
* That gives us a total monthly revenue of **₹12,00,000** (₹4,80,000 from ads + ₹7,20,000 organic). Let's use these numbers to explain how our three metrics interact."

#### [1:00 - 2:15] Concept 1: ROAS (The Efficiency Lens)
* "First, let's look at **ROAS**, or Return on Ad Spend. ROAS answers the question: *'For every rupee I put into the advertising machine, how many rupees does it spit back out?'*
* For EcoBeans, we spent ₹1,20,000 and got ₹4,80,000 in ad revenue. So, $\text{ROAS} = 4,80,000 / 1,20,000 = 4.0$. We got four rupees back for every rupee we spent.
* Why does a brand spending ₹15L/month care about this? Because it determines cash flow efficiency. If you are spending ₹15L and your ROAS is 4.0, you are generating ₹60L in ad revenue. But if your ROAS drops to 1.5, you only make ₹22.5L. When you factor in the cost to buy the coffee, package it, and pay Amazon's fees, a 1.5 ROAS means you are actively losing money.
* What makes a ROAS 'good'? It's entirely dependent on margins. If you sell electronics with a 15% margin, you need a ROAS of 7.0 or higher just to break even. But for EcoBeans, because coffee has high margins (say 60%), a ROAS of 3.0 is actually quite comfortable and profitable."

#### [2:15 - 3:30] Concept 2: ACOS (The Direct Cost Lens & The Inverse Relationship)
* "Next is **ACOS**, or Advertising Cost of Sale. This is the flip side of the coin. If ROAS measures return, ACOS measures *cost*. It asks: *'What percentage of my ad sales did I spend on the ads themselves?'*
* In our EcoBeans example, we spent ₹1,20,000 to get ₹4,80,000 in ad sales. So, $\text{ACOS} = (1,20,000 / 4,80,000) \times 100 = 25\%$. 
* You'll notice immediately that 25% is exactly $1 / 4.0$. ROAS and ACOS are mathematical inverses. A ROAS of 4.0 is a 25% ACOS; a ROAS of 2.0 is a 50% ACOS.
* Now, when is a high ACOS okay? When we launch a new coffee blend, say a 'Vanilla Dark Roast,' nobody knows it exists. It has zero reviews and zero search history. We have to bid aggressively on keywords like 'organic dark roast' to get noticed. We might tolerate an ACOS of 80% or 100% in month one because we're buying visibility and getting our first 100 reviews. That's an investment.
* But if we've been selling our signature blend for a year, and the ACOS is still 55% while our product margin is only 40%, we are losing money on every single sale. That's a red flag."

#### [3:30 - 4:45] Concept 3: TACOS (The Big Picture Health Metric)
* "This brings us to the most important metric: **TACOS**, or Total Advertising Cost of Sale. 
* ACOS can lie to you. It only looks at ad sales. But Amazon ads don't work in isolation. When a customer clicks our ad and buys EcoBeans, our product climbs the organic search results page. More people find us naturally, leading to free, organic sales. This is the 'Amazon Halo Effect.'
* TACOS measures ad spend against *total* revenue. For EcoBeans, our total revenue is ₹12,00,000, and our ad spend is ₹1,20,000. So, $\text{TACOS} = (1,20,000 / 12,00,000) \times 100 = 10\%$.
* Why is a 10% TACOS beautiful? It shows our business is healthy and balanced. 
* Look at what happens over time. Suppose next month, our ad spend stays flat at ₹1,20,000 and our ad sales stay flat at ₹4,80,000. Our ACOS is still 25%.
* However, because of the search rankings we won last month, our organic sales grow from ₹7,20,000 to ₹10,80,000. 
* Our total revenue is now ₹15,60,000. Our new TACOS is $1,20,000 / 15,60,000 = 7.7\%$. 
* Even though our ad campaigns looked exactly the same on paper (ACOS was stuck at 25%), our TACOS dropped from 10% to 7.7%. This tells the founder that the advertising spend is successfully driving organic brand compound growth, making the business more profitable overall."

#### [4:45 - 5:30+] Summary & Conclusion
* "So to wrap it all up:
  * **ROAS** is your immediate campaign efficiency multiplier.
  * **ACOS** is your direct campaign cost percentage.
  * **TACOS** is the ultimate heartbeat monitor of the brand.
* An analyst who only looks at ROAS or ACOS is like a pilot who only looks at the engine RPM. An analyst who looks at TACOS is looking at the altitude and destination. When you connect all three, you can advise a brand not just on how to bid on keywords, but whether they are building a real asset on Amazon.
* That’s my take on these three metrics. Let me know if that makes sense!"
